Overall employment is holding steady, and technology optimists are pointing to it as evidence that fears about automation were overblown. The aggregate number does not tell you what is happening inside it.

When you break employment down by sector, occupation type, and wage band, a more complicated picture emerges. Entry-level white-collar work in several industries has contracted meaningfully. The workers displaced from those roles are not, for the most part, moving into higher-skill adjacent positions.

## What the Research Shows

A working paper from three university economics departments, circulated in March, compared employment transitions from 2023 to 2025 across a sample of 4,000 firms that had deployed large language model tools in their workflows.

The finding that got the most press was the productivity gain — real, significant. The finding that got less coverage: the median entry-level headcount in those firms dropped 22% over two years, while senior-level headcount held flat.

That is not catastrophe. It is also not nothing.

## The Policy Gap

What doesn't exist yet is a coherent policy response to transitions that are happening gradually and unevenly rather than in one visible wave. Gradual and uneven is harder to legislate around than sudden and concentrated.

The conversation is years behind the deployment curve. That gap is worth naming clearly.
