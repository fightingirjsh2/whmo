Ambitious transit plans are easy to announce and hard to fund. The proposal that cleared committee last week has survived more scrutiny than most, and the specifics hold up in ways worth examining.

The core idea — dedicated bus rapid transit lanes on six arterial corridors, with signal priority and off-board fare collection — is proven. Cities that have done this fully (not the half-measures that get called BRT for political credit) see consistent ridership gains within eighteen months.

## The Funding Stack

What makes this proposal unusual is the funding structure. It doesn't rely primarily on federal grants that may or may not materialize. The bulk comes from a value-capture mechanism tied to commercial development within a quarter mile of each station.

That's a real mechanism. It's been tested. The numbers in the feasibility study use conservative ridership projections, which is the right call.

## The Risks

Political durability is the primary risk. Plans like this take four to six years to complete. That's two election cycles. Sustained support across changing administrations is historically the hardest part of infrastructure in this region.

Construction sequencing also matters. If the first corridor takes twice as long as projected — which they usually do — public confidence erodes before the full network can demonstrate its value.

Worth watching. Cautiously optimistic.
