As a relatively new hardcore Destiny 2 player, Destiny 3 has been a topic of conversation as long as I have been active in the Destiny community. While I played Destiny 1, its expansions, and picked up Destiny 2 in Forsaken, I only became a fully invested guardian during the Season of Arrivals, shortly before Beyond Light launched in 2020. I remember cramming to complete as much of the soon-to-be vaulted content during September and October, remiss that I hadn't engaged more with the game earlier so I could have enjoyed that content without the looming spectre of the Destiny Content Vault and gear sunsetting would change the game forever.

By starting in the game when I did, I had a uniquely jealous perspective on the players that had enjoyed the launches of the content that I would lose access to so shortly after falling in love with the world of Destiny 2. At the time I had a very clear vision of how they should have handled content vaulting that would address the technical limitations they had, while allowing new players to join the Destiny universe in a complete state as opposed to the disjointed mess it became. Over the years I have only become more sure that this idea was the correct strategy then, as new players smashed into the brick wall that was the new player experience without a proper campaign, and eventually dried up alltogether, leading to where we are today. I believe this alternative remains the correct strategy today especially in light of the fact that the Destiny universe is effectively done; Destiny 2 is entering maintanence mode as of June 9th, and there is no development of Destiny 3 occuring at Bungie as of writing. While many in the Destiny community are still demanding Sony greenlight Destiny 3 to continue our beloved world, reporting indicates they do not have the appetite to fund a development cycle that would reportedly cost upwards of $500 million dollars at a studio that is undeniably struggling.

I can be a bit verbose in my writing, so enough preamble, lets cut to the chase.

Destiny 2 should have had a rolling sequel schedule that slices off swaths of the live game into evergreen static prequels(?) as the live game continues on. As the game grows larger and more unwieldy to manage, say every 2/3 years when the game would be reaching 5/6 contained expansions, Bungie could split off the oldest 3 expansions into a maintenance mode state, and make major technical overhauls on the live game.

For example, pretend we were never plagued with the Destiny Content Vault in 2020, but rather as of the launch of the Witch Queen expansion, the game would look as follows.

Redwar + Curse of Osiris and Warmind
Forsaken + 3 Seasons
Shadowkeep + 4 Seasons
Beyond Light + 4 Seasons
Witch Queen, Live game

At this point the game may have become truly unwieldy, but could have been solved by not vaulting content, but splitting it off into a Destiny 1 like static game and a live game.

Destiny 2 or Destiny: 2017-2019
Redwar + Curse of Osiris and Warmind
Forsaken + 3 Seasons
Shadowkeep + 4 Seasons

and

Destiny 3 or Destiny Infinity or just... *Destiny*
Beyond Light + 4 Seasons
Witch Queen, Live Game 

In this state, the launch of the Witch Queen (or whatever expansions this coincided with), could launch with significant system overhauls to the technical state of the game such as Beyond Light's lighting overhaul, without having to put significant development effort into upgrading older, less relevant content. It also offers an opportunity to add new interesting gameplay changes like subclass 3.0 or strand without trivializing old content that wasn't built for it. 

At the same time, this would leave older content in a complete, playable state so that new players could enjoy that content before or even after enjoying the live game. Aside from just the historical and onboarding importance of these earlier expansions being playable, it could be fun to add rewards that carry forward into the live game that reward players for stepping back and investing their time in older content.

I am not a game developer and don't pretend to understand every challenge that this would bring into developing and managing the game, but I don't think it seems exceptionally unreasonable, especially juxtaposed with the rewards it would bring.

How would this be beneficial?
1. Players could enjoy the complete Destiny experience from beginning to end, including seasonal content
2. New players would have a much better onboarding experience being able to play every aspect of the Destiny story
3. Allow new and old players to experience the different gameplay over the years
	1. As an example, Destiny 2 would be left in the subclass 2.0 world, allowing players to experience this period as a time capsule to what the game once was
4. Keep the live game small
	1. This would add new challenges, but it would keep the currently maintained live game in a more manageable state
5. Natural gear reset points
	1. Instead of perpetual power creep, these points could be used to sunset gear by simply leaving it with the expansion that it came from

If anyone ever actually reads this article, I'm sure there are many implementation questions that will arise, so I'll try to answer some anticipated questions below.

Q&A
Q: Would non-exotic gear come forward with you or be sunset?
A: For non-exotic gear, I think each new slice would be a good time to sunset old gear. While everyone hates sunsetting (understandable, especially with how it was handled in Destiny 2), I do think its important for keeping the sandbox moving forward without struggling to manage legacy gear. For a game like Destiny to succeed, its important for there to be motivation to seek out new gear, and the two main ways to achieve this is sunsetting older gear or power creeping it with more powerful new gear. The powercreep route can end up seriously problamatic for the sandbox long term, making it difficult to design activities that challenge players without resorting to gimmicks that take the magic out of the gameplay.

Q: Would Eververse items and cosmetics come forward with you?
A: I think cosmetics such as weapon ornaments, paid armor ornaments, emblems, ships, ghosts, and sparrows would all move forward with your account across games. This brings some unity to your collection that makes you feel that playing any edition of Destiny over time is valuable and you are building out your collection. For example, I think it would be interesting in 2026 to go back 2 games to grind Season of the Forge activities for missing emblems you could bring forward into the live game, reducing some of the FOMO games like Destiny have that can alienate new players since they feel like they are so far behind, why bother? Of course there would be edge cases as systems change that may be left behind, for example if some day ghost projections were no longer supported in the live game, so be it.

Q: How would exotic gear be handled?
A: Exotics bring the hardest question to answer. An ever growing pool of exotics makes the sandbox harder to manage and can be overwhelming at some point, but the sale of paid exotic ornaments makes it hard to not bring them forward. While an imperfect solution, I think you just bring them forward in the live game because the pros outweigh the cons. Similar to emblems and ships etcetera, it would be an opportunity to have cross game progression, allowing a player to go back and play old content to acquire exotics the way they were supposed to be gained and brought forward across your account. I think it would be valuable to be able to go back and play exotic missions and campaign quests that give you the exotic weapon rather than purchase it from a kiosk. Organically building our your accounts collection is one of the most beautiful parts of the Destiny experience.

Q: Would there only be a few Raids, Dungeons and Strikes in the live game?
A: Just because we're slicing off large chunks of the live game, doesn't mean nothing can carry forward. I think this could be handled the way it is now. Ideally you would leave all Raids and Dungeons in the live game. These would also be in their sliced version, for example Last Wish would exist in Both Destiny 2017-2019, and in the live game, allowing players to enjoy it with multiple sandboxes, not unlike the returned Destiny 1 raids into Destiny 2.
Strikes are a harder challenge because they require updating parts of the sliced destinations, which to be fair is something they do now to some extent. Maybe that's considered worth it, or maybe they are kept with their expansion and we get more than one new strike per year in this system.

Q: How would you handle seasonal content, such as the helm changes between seasons in the past?
A: This is an opportunity to address an idea that I have seen floating around the community for a few years, legacy seasonal rotations. You could have monthly seasonal rotations to simulate the experience of the seasons launching. Alternatively they could be retrofitted to have helm instances for each season so they all work concurrently. Of course if this was originally designed into the game maybe it simply wouldn't have been a problem, but as it stands now there are a few options to address this.

Q: Do you have a longer term view of how this would look?
A: Here's an example of how this might look after many years, for example in 2027.
Destiny 1:
Vanilla + The Dark Below and House of Wolves
The Taken King
Rise of Iron + Age of Triumph

Destiny 2 or Destiny 2017-2019:
Redwar + Curse of Osiris and Warmind
Forsaken + 3 Seasons
Shadowkeep + 4 Seasons

Destiny 3 or Destiny 2020-2024:
Beyond Light + 4 Seasons
Witch Queen + 4 Seasons
Lightfall + 4 Seasons
The Final Shape + 4 Seasons

Destiny Live game:
Edge of Fate + Ash and Iron
Renegades + Shadow and Order
Shattered Cycle + Major Update
The Alchemist + Major Update
The story continues...

I think this is a nice setup. For new players, they can go back and experience the full Destiny story, enjoy some of the games best content, and earn gear and cosmetics that carry forward into the live game. The ability to watch your account and characters grow over time is quintessential Destiny, and its only possible by keeping older content available. Just as importantly, older players should be able to have access to the content they paid for, both to revisit and because its just the right way to run a business. For Bungie, the ability to focus their developers on making new content and major system upgrades within a more narrow scope of content should improve the speed they move, limit the mistakes that slip into the game, and reduce development costs, which is especially important in this new era of game-making where costs are ballooning. 

Its all academic at this point because Destiny 2 is over, but in a future where Destiny continues in some way I think this system could provide a method to avoid mistakes of the past. Someday if Destiny 3 is announced, updating Destiny 2 into a complete state could be a good prequel for players to enjoy in the build up to the launch of Destiny 3. It may be unrealistic since studio resources would be and should be focused on the new game content, but it may be a good peace offering to those whose faith in Bungie was shaken by the Destiny content vault.

Who knows what the future of Destiny is, if there is any future at all. I think that Bungie would be wise to base the future of Destiny on improving a streamlining Destiny 2, rather than reinventing the wheel. Reducing cost is the name of the game if this franchise is too survive and I think a ground up rethinking of the franchise is going to be prohibitively expensive, and Destiny 2, while flawed, is awesome at its core.
