The argument against the pitch clock, before it was implemented, was that it would address a symptom while leaving the underlying cause untouched. Games were long because of the number of pitching changes, not because pitchers were standing on the mound thinking. Speeding up the between-pitch interval wouldn't matter.

That argument was partially right and mostly wrong.

## What Changed

Average game time dropped by around 25 minutes per game — more than most analysts projected. The reduction came from multiple sources, not just the pitch clock itself. Batters stepping out less, fewer mound visits, tighter execution of pitching changes. The rule changed the rhythm and the rhythm changed behavior downstream.

Strikeout rates also declined, which was not predicted by most models. The leading explanation: batters have less time to work counts, which means they're making contact earlier in at-bats. That contact is producing more balls in play. That's producing more action. That action is producing more engagement, and the ratings numbers, while not dramatic, are trending correctly.

## What Didn't Change

The games that involve lots of pitching changes are still long. A nine-pitcher game is still a long game.

The strategic response — fewer pitching changes per game — is starting to show up in the data, and it's producing more interesting late-inning situations. That's the second-order effect that makes this worth a closer look.

The clock worked. Whether it was the right intervention is still an open question. It worked, though.
